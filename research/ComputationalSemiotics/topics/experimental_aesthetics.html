Prior Work

It all started back in High School, when my Artificial Intelligence teacher had us find local minnimas of a function using a simplex method. "Couldn't a computer be used to visualize the function that we are optimizing", I thought. It seemed strange to me that we were optimizing a function that I could not see and that I couldn't empirically check to see whether my solutions were sensible. I assumed that a standard programming language would have a function that took in a mathematical function and mapped it to every pixel. I didn't know that shaders existed back then, and I guess I pretend that shaders still don't exist now, I went about programming my very first image renderer using idiosyncratic names like 'colorCalculator'.

Most of the images presented here were created though old fashion mathematics program in text editors using programming languages.

I worked out some very idiosyncratic geometry calculations back then. I spent a lot of time trying to represent objects that I liked such as musical instruments. It was only later in college that I learned that if I am going to put in the effort to hand code arbitrary geometry, I should try to go for something wacky that couldn't be done using more traditional methods. I realize that more now. I believe that these attempts due have value as conceptual art, where the idea is that a person can encode them strictly through mathematics and typing, rather than by painting or using more traditional tools.
It took me a full year to encode all of the geometry for this english horn. I had no helpful programs, just a text editor and a go button. I learned many techniques such as rendering only a sliver of the image, commenting out certain features, etc. I guess nobody except me knows how much effort I put into making this image and that I really didn't have any external tools whatsoever. I hope that someday my efforts will be worth it. Maybe they already are, since I have the pleasure of understanding how computations work.
In college, I kept making images. Here is one about the patterns formed by the structure of a roll of tickets. I was starting to get a bit of mathematical nuance into my system at this point.

Development

Although my journey began several years ago, this page details my formal exploration into image making during October, November, and December, 2017. Its been a rough, tough, and wild ride.

Image Thoughts Links
I wanted to create an image making technique based on a model of human perception, where everyhing gets decomposed into a plane, which is the extent to which we can dicern spatial information. I imagined that perhaps shape was seen as variations of normals and maybe our eyes discretize them into regions of equal normal in 3 dimensions. The sphere came out roughly like I imagined that it would, although I would personally prefer more prescision and think that the "biological impressions" of the work lend some texture, but do not aid in communicating the message that I was going for.
I tried the technique out on a hand mesh, but it came out technically sensible, but aesthetically unreadable. I suppose that it would eventually work given a subdivision model of a hand, but that is a research project for future years.
After I finished working with meshes and line divisions, I tried to get closer to visualizing paths, which are how the rational part of my mind sees the world, where it models the actions and behavior that it sees into systemic structures. Later on I was advised to focus more on what I saw, rather than what I thought that I saw. I made this image at a healthcare makerthon, where we were tasked with reinventing the EKG data visualization that is commonly used to read how well a person's heart is performing. I mad this image that shows the circulations of the electrical waved through the various parts of the heart. With more work it could be cleaned up and aligned with data streams, but for now I'm glad of how it worked out. An interesting note is that this was my first experiement with dividing up an image frame using relationships, rather than hardcoded values. Each chamber has no definate size, but they are defined as subdivisions and orthogonal relationships between one another. This technique was used in the Woodhull image, which you shall see later.
Back to biology land, I thought that I could overcome the limitations of the pixel by creating 9 impression groupings that show a both a value and a direction. I did a lot of brainstorming and musing on the nature of impressions, feelings, meanings, and social norms in regards to tiny circles. If there is a practical internal theoretical insight that I personally gained from this process, I don't think that I was able to translate it into a surprising revelation on the image plane.
Staying on the subject of directionality, I continued to investigate vector fields on the plane. I desired to figure out ways to fill in and texture a space I wanted something that had directionality, depth, and was modular in that it was not problematic if parts were moved or blocked off. Sometimes I think that I've spent too much of my life fixated on 2D textures, but I think it will pay off someday when I figure out my projective geometry work. Along the way, I'm gaining more experience with Vector Algebra, including gradient free circulation fields, that can be used to conserve fluid.
I continued along the noise field path, until I started using simpler forms and eventually came to this carpet texture, which was a result of a conservative vector field and getting the directionality procuedure just right. I wish that it was brighter though. Brightness is something that I neeed to work on in my image making work, because too bright and the piece loses details and jars the eye, too dark and the details are not visible. Also, since I read the texture as a carpet, I will need to keep going to find a method of directionality that is beautiful to me, but is more like a master craftsman's paint stroke, rather than a carpet simulation. I like conservative vector fields, because like the division of a plane, they are ideally aesthetically interesting, without having a definate form, but rather filling an entire region with interesting detail.
Now that I was happy for the most part with my carpet texture, I moved on to form. I worked out the mathematics for constructing a spline and imagining the interior as a vector field that flows along the curve. In order to visualize something specific, I took a traffic intersection pattern, where 2 four lane roads meet with sidewalks and colored all of the allowed potential paths green, those that must yield yellow, and those encumbered by the flow of traffic red. The blending was not perfect and I think I blended these together using Paint.net
After conceptualizing the intersection of paths, I worked out the mathematics for seemlessly blending impressions of various colored paths in 1 layer computationally. Later I've gone back and realized my vision for illustrating the potentialities and rules of a traffic intersection consisting of 4 lanes of automobile traffic and sidewalks for pedestrians. This is an example of an image that I can make using my path mathematics, that I could not make by compositing and blending carpet textures in Paint.net such as in the image previous. This particular style of overlaid blending allows for the paths of traffic flow to be perceived as they are with their spatial extents and their color, rather than a wash of blending like that found in the previous image. I might want to reapply this technique to some of the later images to solve the problem of light oversaturating to white.

The discretization and concavities of the curves form barriers where they bend.

For this image, I tried a large format rendering to test out whether I could keep the illumination reasonable at higher levels. The consequence is that the oversaturated regions of the point lights diminish in size relative to the entire image. I like how it turned out.

Sketch Thoughts Links
People see objects subjectively in the world. This subjective nature obscures the way that nature transmits light through photons, at least as far as our current understanding goes. This subjective view serves to obscure the objective workings of nature. I value subjective perception, but I also value understanding nature. I think that people need to know both and that mapping complicated representations of objective models to subjectively pallatable forms will make understanding accessible to more people. Common aesthetic taste is a social norm of communication as much as common phrases. Why not conform our visual examples to the joyful aesthetics of the people, rather than the aesthetics of current computer generated general use imagery such as powerpoint based lecture slides.
This image depicts forms using a common lighting model approximation from the field of computer graphics called 'Lambertian Reflectance'. This model is ubiquitous in 3D modeling software and common visualizations of 3D shape. Many tool users have a parameter to control lambertian refelctance, but have little knowledge of what it means and why it produces the given lighting approximation.
Photons travel through space, each one with a specific direction and hue. They emit from a light source, bounce around, and may eventually hit one of our eyes. We can't see them swarming around us, because we only see a sliver of the paths that hit our eyeballs, therefore this representation shows what our minds, but not our eyes can see about the behavior of photons.
Close together, these photons often travel in roughly the same direction in beams of light. These beams eventually hit an object surface, which locally looks like a plane under the lambertian model. Actual surfaces have other details such as roughness, atomic energy states, etc. that could be captured in other lighting models. Notice that a cross section of a beam is spread out over a larger area when it hits a surface at a less perpendicular angle. This purely geometric phenomena is called the Law of Cosines.
Under the Lambertian model, light bounces in a uniformly random direction along the hemisphere of the normal that it hits a surface. This is known as diffuse reflection and it is responsible for evenly scattering photons in white walled rooms.
The subjective appearance of lambertian reflectance modelled objects is due to a combination of the law of cosines and diffuse reflection. When combined, no matter the angle we look at a diffuse surface, the amount of photons coming to our eyes will be proportional to the amount of photons hitting that surface at that location, which is dependant on the angle a beam of light hits the surface. This is why an objects's surface darkens when it faces away from a light source.
